# TRUST HUB Governance Model

**Version 1.0**  
**Effective Date:** February 2026  
**Authority:** TRUST Infrastructure Constitution (Section 1)

---

## Executive Summary

TRUST HUB is a **curated infrastructure**, not a free marketplace. This document defines how that curation operates—the decisions TRUST HUB makes, the criteria it applies, and the limits it respects.

The governance model rests on three foundations:
1. **Centralized authority** exercised transparently and within clear bounds
2. **Structural protections** against abuse of that authority
3. **Accountability mechanisms** that make misuse visible and costly

This is not decentralized governance (which spreads accountability to nothing) or laissez-faire operation (which abdicates responsibility). It is **responsible curation** with explicit authority and explicit limits.

## Section 1: Account Approval Model

### 1.1 Purpose of Approval

Account approval serves three functions:
- **Verification:** Confirm participant identity and basic legitimacy
- **Commitment:** Secure participant agreement to infrastructure rules and arbitration
- **Safety:** Prevent known bad actors from entering the network

Approval is **not** about:
- Judging capability (we match; we don't pre-qualify expertise)
- Controlling who can participate (low bar, high transparency)
- Creating scarcity (we want network effects; more good actors strengthen the system)

### 1.2 Two-Track Approval Process

**EXPERTS (Service Providers)**

Requirement: Validation Call (20-30 minutes)

The call covers:
- Identity verification (government ID, video confirmation of name match)
- Business registration verification (company number, tax registration if applicable)
- Communication skills assessment (can they articulate what they do?)
- Arbitration acknowledgment (explicit understanding of dispute process)
- Red flag detection (obvious criminal intent, predatory language, unrealistic claims)

Outcome options:
- **APPROVED:** Standard account activation. Expert can receive matches.
- **CONDITIONAL APPROVAL:** Approved with restrictions (e.g., "max €2K project value until 3 successful deliveries"). Allows participation while demonstrating reliability.
- **REJECTED:** Account not created. Reason given in writing. Right of appeal after 90 days with new information.

Call conducted by trained TRUST infrastructure staff, not algorithms. Judgment applied within documented criteria.

---

**COMPANIES (Project Posters)**

Requirement: Validation Call (15-20 minutes) + Financial Verification

The call covers:
- Company identity verification (registration documents, main contact verification)
- Financial verification (bank account confirmation, typically 3-month statement to verify operational funds)
- Project scope assessment (can they articulate what they need?)
- Arbitration acknowledgment (explicit understanding of dispute process)
- Budget verification (claimed budget must be realistic for stated scope)

Outcome options: APPROVED / CONDITIONAL APPROVAL / REJECTED (same structure as experts)

Financial verification serves one purpose: confirming the company can pay what it commits. This is a **safety measure for experts**, not a judgment on the company's worth.

### 1.3 Minimum Access Criteria

| Category | Requirement | Purpose |
|----------|-------------|---------|
| Identity | Government ID (verified name match) | Prevent multiple accounts, enable accountability |
| Business Registration | Valid company/self-employed registration | Baseline legitimacy |
| Communication | Native/fluent language match (service language) | Enable clear contracts |
| Financial (Companies) | Verified bank account | Confirm ability to pay |
| Arbitration Acknowledgment | Explicit written understanding of dispute process | Binding consent |
| No Criminal Red Flags | No current fraud/embezzlement investigations | Safety threshold |
| No Pattern History | Not previously banned from TRUST for misconduct | Repeat offense filter |

### 1.4 Right to Refuse Access

TRUST HUB reserves the right to refuse account creation or activation in the following cases:

1. **Failed verification** (identity cannot be confirmed, documents are fraudulent)
2. **Misrepresentation** (false business registration, false financial status)
3. **Red flag behavior** (during call: threats, solicitation of illegal activity, explicit predatory intent)
4. **Previous misconduct** (previously banned from TRUST for arbitration violations or fraud)
5. **Scope mismatch** (requested service scope incompatible with TRUST's infrastructure—e.g., illegal services, government contract work, activities requiring professional licensing beyond TRUST's oversight)

**Refusal does NOT require:**
- Detailed explanation of "why you're bad" (that invites legal argumentation)
- Proof of harm (prevention > remediation)
- Board vote or lengthy process (infrastructure discretion within stated criteria)

**Refusal DOES provide:**
- Written reason (general category, not detailed dossier)
- 90-day right of appeal with new information
- No permanent ban (re-application possible after 12 months if initial reason has changed)

This is consistent with how platforms operate: reasonable gatekeeping to maintain system integrity.

## Section 2: Content and Posting Approval Model

### 2.1 What Content Is Permitted

**PROJECTS/POSTINGS:**

✅ **Permitted content:**
- Service requests within stated scope of TRUST (human expertise delivery, remote or hybrid delivery)
- Clear deliverables with defined timelines and budgets
- Specific expertise requirements (do not post generic requests for "help with my business")
- Professional scope (legal, technical, business, creative, etc.)
- Confidential work (NDA projects are permitted; TRUST handles confidentiality in arbitration)
- Revised/updated postings (posting same project multiple times is allowed with 7-day intervals)

**❌ Prohibited content:**
- Illegal services (hitman, fraud services, drug synthesis, counterfeit goods, etc.)
- Licensed professional services requiring credentials beyond TRUST's oversight (medical diagnosis, legal representation in court, structural engineering for critical infrastructure)
- Unlimited/unclear scope ("I need business help" without specifics)
- Predatory pricing ("I'll pay €50 for a complete business plan")
- Harassment/abuse projects ("I want to research ways to contact my ex-partner's employer")
- Multilevel marketing recruitment
- Research requiring informed consent from human subjects (requires IRB approval)

**EXPERT PROFILES AND POSITIONING:**

✅ **Permitted:**
- Detailed service descriptions with examples
- Case study references (with confidentiality respected through generalization)
- Credentials, certifications, published work
- Honest pricing and scope clarity
- Professional development timeline (e.g., "3 years in web development, now specializing in e-commerce")

**❌ Prohibited:**
- Guarantees of outcomes ("I will increase your sales 300%" without contract specificity)
- Misrepresented credentials (claiming certifications not held)
- Competitive attacks ("I'm better than [named competitor]")
- Implicit guarantees that shift liability ("I only work with serious clients" → implies you're not serious)
- Deceptive pricing ("First consultation €20" without noting scope, then charging €200+ for followup)

### 2.2 Enforcement: What TRUST Does

**CONTENT REVIEW PROCESS:**

1. **Submission:** Posting submitted by account holder
2. **Automated screening:** Filter for obvious prohibited content (keywords, patterns)
3. **If flagged:** Human review within 24 hours
4. **If approved:** Published to matching pool
5. **If rejected:** Written notice with general reason ("does not meet scope requirements" or "uses prohibited language") + right of appeal with revision

Response timeline: 24-48 hours for approval/rejection.

Appeal process: Revise and resubmit, or escalate to Governance Committee if requester believes rejection was unjustified.

**INFRASTRUCTURE ROLE IN MAINTAINING STANDARD:**

The infrastructure curates **scope**, not **quality**. Key distinction:

| TRUST Curates | Individual Experts Determine |
|---|---|
| Is this a valid TRUST project? | Will I be good at this? |
| Is scope clear? | Is my price fair? |
| Does it violate rules? | Am I the right fit? |
| Is it honest marketing? | What's my personal brand? |

**Example:** 
- TRUST rejects: "Become a millionaire in 30 days for €50" (violates honesty standard)
- TRUST does NOT reject: "Digital marketing consulting, €500/project, 2-week delivery" (that's expert judgment; if it fails, arbitration handles it)

**Enforcement triggers:**
- Expert persistently posts unclear scope → conversation required
- Expert uses deceptive pricing patterns → account warning → conditional approval restriction → potential suspension
- Expert lists fake credentials → credential verification → potential rejection

### 2.3 Dispute: Content Approval Decisions

If account holder disagrees with content rejection or removal:

1. **Written appeal** within 7 days citing reason for disagreement
2. **Governance Committee review** (independent from original decision-maker)
3. **Restore or maintain** decision with explanation
4. **Escalation option:** If pattern of disputes, escalate to Constitution-bound Arbitration Panel for precedent-setting case

This prevents arbitrary censorship while maintaining infrastructure standards.

## Section 3: Supervision Model

### 3.1 Infrastructure Access to Conversational Data

TRUST infrastructure has **access to conversations** between experts and companies for the duration of a project and 30 days after completion.

**This access serves three specific functions:**

1. **Fraud Detection** (Ongoing)
   - Monitor for early signs of scam: pricing manipulation, credential misuse, pressure tactics
   - Action: Flag parties for conversation, issue warning, restrict account, or terminate project (rare)

2. **Abuse Prevention** (Ongoing)
   - Monitor for harassment, coercion, threats, sexual content
   - Action: Immediately escalate to law enforcement, suspend accounts, preserve evidence

3. **Arbitration Support** (On Dispute)
   - If dispute opens, infrastructure reviews conversation to understand context
   - Provides conversation history to arbitrator (redacted of unrelated private information)
   - Clarifies facts ("on March 12, the expert said 'delivery by March 20'" vs. party claims)

**After 30 days post-completion:** Conversations archived and de-indexed. Available only:
- For arbitration if dispute opened during or 30 days after project
- For law enforcement with subpoena
- For legitimate appeal of arbitration decision

### 3.2 Explicit Boundaries: What Supervision Is NOT

**TRUST does NOT:**

- **Monitor tone** (two parties being frustrated is normal; infrastructure only intervenes if boundaries crossed)
- **Settle disagreements about work quality** during execution ("He's being slow" → not infrastructure's job unless it's pattern of delay with malicious intent)
- **Require pre-approval of messages** (this isn't a nanny system; accountability comes through arbitration)
- **Share conversation data with third parties** (never sold, never used for marketing, never shared with other experts)
- **Use conversation data to train algorithms** about individuals (no behavioral profiling of specific parties)
- **Intervene in payment disputes** during project (this is arbitration scope, not supervision scope)

**Clear statement:** Supervision is **threat detection**, not **relationship management**. We're watching for fraud, abuse, coercion—not commenting on professionalism or fit.

### 3.3 Transparency of Supervision

Parties are informed that:
- Conversations are monitored for safety and fraud (stated in account agreement)
- Specific monitoring triggers are defined (fraud patterns, abuse, threat language)
- Data is not used for algorithm training or behavioral analysis
- After 30 days, conversation is archived and inaccessible except for dispute/law enforcement

No surprise. No hidden surveillance. Framed as infrastructure safety, not control.

## Section 4: Matching Model

### 4.1 Why Matching Is Infrastructure Function

**TRUST does not operate a free marketplace.** Instead:

- Companies post projects but **do not directly choose experts**
- Experts apply but **are not automatically matched**
- TRUST **curates the match** based on explicit criteria

Why? Because:
1. **Fraud reduction:** Experts with criminal history don't automatically match high-value projects
2. **Outcome quality:** Companies with vague needs don't match experts in high-specialization domains
3. **Fairness:** Newer experts get exposure instead of top-rated experts monopolizing all work
4. **Network effects:** Strategic matching builds cohesive community instead of fragmented marketplace

### 4.2 Automatic (Algorithmic) Matching Is NOT Used

Expert matching does not follow algorithmic rules like:
- "Match the highest-rated expert with the highest budget"
- "Match based on keyword similarity in profile and project"
- "Match based on geographic proximity"

Why algorithms fail in this context:
- **Gaming:** Experts game keywords; companies game scope language
- **Bias:** Algorithms lock in initial patterns (first experts get more exposure)
- **Quality blindness:** Algorithm can't assess fit quality, only profile match

### 4.3 Human-Centered Matching Criteria

Matching process:

1. **Project Analysis** (TRUST staff)
   - Is scope clear and legitimate? (Yes/No)
   - What expertise is genuinely needed?
   - What's the risk profile? (Budget, timeline, complexity)

2. **Pool Identification** (TRUST staff + algorithm)
   - Filter to experts with relevant background
   - Exclude: newly approved (< 1 project complete), conditional approvals with project limits
   - For high-risk projects (€10K+, complex scope): require 3+ successful project history
   - Result: 8-15 candidate experts

3. **Human Selection** (TRUST matching specialist)
   - Interview candidates (if necessary for complex projects)
   - Assess communication style fit with company communication style
   - Balance: diverse expert pool (not just top-rated) with competence (minimum threshold)
   - Consider: Does this expert have specific need right now? Is company's timeline urgent? Are both parties aligned?

4. **Presentation & Selection**
   - Company receives 3-5 expert profiles (not 100; not 1)
   - Includes: credentials, example projects, approach, pricing
   - Company chooses from curated set OR requests different candidates
   - Expert accepts or declines engagement

### 4.4 Infrastructure Intervention in Matching

TRUST infrastructure **can intervene** to improve quality:

**Scenario A:** Expert is technically qualified but communication is poor (profile is vague, responses are slow)
- **Action:** Conversation with expert about communication expectations. If not improved, excluded from next matching round.

**Scenario B:** Company scope is vague; candidate matching pool is weak
- **Action:** Request company clarify scope before proceeding. Refusal = project not listed.

**Scenario C:** Expert repeatedly misses promised delivery windows with new clients
- **Action:** Expert limited to lower-value projects or shorter timelines until pattern improves.

**Scenario D:** Company is seeking expert for work requiring licensing (tax advice from someone without CPA) but doesn't acknowledge the licensing gap
- **Action:** Project flagged in system. Company notified of licensing requirement. Experts informed they should clarify they do not hold this license.

**Scenario E:** Expert has valuable niche (e.g., Russian-language copywriting for startups) but low overall rating because previous projects were outside niche
- **Action:** Matched to projects in niche specifically, re-rated in that context.

These interventions serve quality and safety. They are not:
- Punishment
- Arbitrary
- Final (expert can improve and re-engage)
- Hidden (reasoned in writing to party if questioned)

## Section 5: Authority Limits

### 5.1 What TRUST Does NOT Control

**DELIVERY OUTCOME:**

TRUST does not decide whether work is "good enough."

- Expert delivers what contract specified.
- Company evaluates if it meets their needs.
- If company claims it doesn't meet specification: arbitration assesses contract compliance.
- If company is dissatisfied with quality but contract was met: **not TRUST's business**. This is why rating/reputation exists—quality concerns are market signals.

**Example:** Company hires designer for website. Designer delivers website on time, on spec. Company thinks design is ugly. This is not an arbitration case; this is company's risk for not specifying design requirements clearly. Company can rate designer poorly and avoid future matches.

---

**PROFESSIONAL RESPONSIBILITY:**

TRUST does not absorb the expert's professional responsibility.

- Expert contracted is responsible for their own work quality.
- Expert is responsible for disclosing relevant limitations (can't code in Python if project requires Python).
- TRUST does not guarantee expert's competence; TRUST curates pool (medium assurance, not absolute assurance).
- If expert's negligence causes harm: expert is liable. TRUST's liability is limited to arbitration arbitration-process fairness, not work quality.

**Example:** Expert claims expertise in SEO but applies outdated tactics that get company's site penalized. Expert is responsible for damage. TRUST's role is limited to: Did expert claim this expertise? (Yes) → expert is responsible. TRUST does not cover damage.

---

**PRICING:**

TRUST does not set or control prices.

- Expert sets own rates
- Company negotiates or accepts
- TRUST reviews for pattern abuse (expert charged €50 for 40 hours → conversation required) but does not intervene in individual pricing

**Exception:** If pattern is exploitative (expert consistently charges €10/hour to vulnerable populations), TRUST can require account change or restrict to specific project values.

---

**SCOPE OF ENGAGEMENT:**

TRUST does not determine what experts must do.

- Expert can decline projects for any reason
- Expert can set working hours, communication frequency, revision limits
- Company can request specific terms; expert can counter-offer
- TRUST intervenes only if contract becomes impossible (e.g., expert demands payment upfront for full-month engagement with no milestone structure; company demands 24/7 support for €500)

---

**METHOD AND PROCESS:**

TRUST does not specify how expert works.

- Expert can use own tools, own workflow, own process
- Expert can subcontract (if contract permits) or solo
- Expert can request specific feedback cadence or silence between checkpoints
- Company describes outcome; expert chooses path to outcome

---

**OFFLINE DISPUTES:**

TRUST does not mediate conflicts that occur outside the platform.

- Company and expert arrange private dinner to discuss project: that's offline
- Conflict arises; parties want TRUST arbitration: TRUST will not arbitrate offline disputes
- This prevents TRUST from being drawn into personal conflicts camouflaged as "professional disputes"

---

### 5.2 What TRUST IS Responsible For

1. **Arbitration fairness:** If dispute reaches arbitration, process is fair, arbiter is qualified, decision is reasoned
2. **Fraud prevention:** TRUST actively screens for and removes fraudsters
3. **Abuse prevention:** TRUST prevents harassment, threats, coercion
4. **Contract clarity:** TRUST requires clear scope and deliverables before project starts
5. **Participant capability:** TRUST vets that participants meet minimum standards (verified identity, basic business legitimacy)
6. **Escrow safety:** Funds are held securely; not misused by TRUST; released per contract
7. **Dispute timeline:** Arbitration happens within stated windows (72 hours for transactional, 1-2 weeks for behavioral)
8. **Precedent publication:** Decisions are published to show pattern (prevents secret favoritism)

## Section 6: Protections Against Abuse of Power

Curation power is real power. TRUST infrastructure has authority that could be abused. This section describes how abuse is prevented **structurally**, not just promised.

### 6.1 Structural Safeguard 1: Recusal

**TRUST founder and governance team do NOT:**
- Operate as experts in the system (no personal accounts to receive matches)
- Arbitrate disputes they're involved in (even indirectly)
- Make account decisions based on personal preference

**Enforcement:** If founder is accused of conflict in account decision, case escalates to external Governance Committee (not founder-controlled).

---

### 6.2 Structural Safeguard 2: Randomized Review

**Major decisions are not made by single person:**

- Account rejection of €5K+ expert: reviewed by Governance Committee (3 people, rotate)
- Content removal of dispute-triggering posts: reviewed independently before removal order issued
- Matching intervention for account restrictions: reviewed by separate person than original account approver

**Enforcement:** Track who made what decision. If pattern emerges (Person A rejects 80% of applications, far higher than team average), escalate to audit.

---

### 6.3 Structural Safeguard 3: Appeal Rights

**Every major decision has appeal process:**

1. **Account rejection:** Appeal within 90 days with new information
2. **Content removal:** Appeal to Governance Committee within 7 days
3. **Matching intervention (conditional approval):** Appeal after 3 projects with improved metrics
4. **Arbitration decision:** Appeal if process was violated (not if you just dislike outcome)

Appeal goes to **different person/committee** than original decision.

---

### 6.4 Structural Safeguard 4: Published Criteria

**All decisions are made against published criteria (not hidden reasoning):**

- Account approval: criteria published (Section 1.3)
- Content rejection: reasons are general categories (not subjective judgment)
- Matching interventions: stated reason given to party ("lower-value projects while improving delivery consistency")
- Arbitration: full decisions published (with confidentiality where needed)

**Enforcement:** If criteria change, amendment is published 30 days before taking effect. Retroactive rule-making is prohibited.

---

### 6.5 Structural Safeguard 5: External Audit

**Annual independent audit of:**
- Account decisions (are rejections proportional? Is there bias toward repeat parties?)
- Content decisions (is content removal evenly applied? Disproportionate pattern?)
- Matching decisions (do repeated matches to same expert suggest favoritism?)
- Arbitration decisions (are outcomes consistent? Is precedent being followed?)

**Auditor:** Independent third party, not hired by TRUST for other work, reports publicly (not to founder alone).

**Findings:** Published annually (with confidential individual case details redacted). If systemic problems found, action plan required within 30 days.

---

### 6.6 Structural Safeguard 6: Founder Recusal from Dispute Arbitration

**Founder is explicitly prohibited from arbitrating disputes**, even high-profile ones.

Why? Because founder has institutional interest in:
- Protecting system reputation (might rule toward TRUST interest, not fair judgment)
- Favoring repeat parties (repeat parties strengthen network effects)
- Avoiding appeals (controversial decisions get appealed; founder might rule to avoid appeal workload)

Founder's role: **Culture keeper and process designer**, not arbitration arbiter.

---

### 6.7 Monitoring: Detecting Abuse

**System logs enable detection of:**
- Repeated account rejections by single person (toward specific profile: all women, all non-native speakers, all startups?)
- Matching patterns that favor repeat parties
- Content removal that targets specific groups
- Arbitration history of specific arbiters (80% of decisions favor experts? 90% favor companies?)

**Quarterly review:** Governance Committee reviews these metrics. Anomalies escalated. Patterns trigger audit.

---

### 6.8 Punishment: Consequences of Abuse

**If governance abuse is found:**

1. **Minor (one-off, corrected immediately):** Coaching, process improvement, monitoring
2. **Moderate (pattern, but no harm):** Account restriction (person removed from specific decisions), external supervision
3. **Serious (pattern + documented harm):** Removal from governance role, potential legal action, public announcement

**Victim remedies:** If person was harmed by abuse (e.g., wrongfully rejected, can prove economic harm), eligible for:
- Decision reversal (account approved, content restored)
- Compensation (TRUST covers documented losses)
- Right to retrial of any dispute that was affected

**Public accountability:** Serious abuse findings are published (with parties' names) in Annual Governance Report.

## Section 7: Implementation Timeline

### Month 1-2: Foundation
- Finalize approval criteria and call script
- Train approval specialists
- Develop content review guidelines
- Test supervision monitoring system

### Month 2-3: Soft Launch
- Begin account approvals with new criteria
- Test matching process with 20-30 early projects
- Gather feedback on approval process (is 24-48 hour timeline working?)
- Refine matching selection criteria

### Month 3-6: Scale
- Scale approval to 100+ monthly new accounts
- Expand matching to 100% of projects
- Implement quarterly audits internally
- Begin public communication of decisions

### Month 6+: Maturity
- Hire external auditor for annual review
- Publish decisions and precedents
- Establish Governance Committee (5 people rotating)
- Continuous monitoring and improvement

## Section 8: Conclusion: Why This Matters

Curation is **not** oppressive control. Curation is **responsible stewardship**.

**The difference:**

| Control (Bad) | Curation (Good) |
|---|---|
| Reject without reason | Reject with published criteria |
| Change rules retroactively | Publish rule changes 30 days ahead |
| Single person decides all | Multiple people, rotated reviews |
| Appeal impossible | Appeal always available |
| Hidden decisions | Published decisions |
| No oversight | External audit |

TRUST HUB's governance model asserts authority clearly and limits it structurally.

This is how you build **trust in the infrastructure itself**—not by being weak, but by being strong *and* accountable.
